1. Why?

South central region of Bangladesh is characterised as hydro-morphologically dynamic area as major river the Ganges, The Brahmaputra and The Meghna join together and find their way to the Bay of Bengal through this region. Amtali Upazilla in Barguna district located in the south central hydrological region of Bangladesh is categorized as exposed coastal upazila comprises of only one agro-ecological region named the Ganges Tidal Floodplain. The land type of this area is mainly medium high land where the maximum flooding depth is about 90 cm during the monsoon season. Sea level rise impacts become reality in this area as historical observation shows that almost on the coastline the rate of water level rise is found to be 0.53 cm/year during 1977-2002 also projected to rise by 35 cm end of this century. Though the water and sanitation coverage of Bangladesh has significantly increased but the issue of WASH resiliency and sustainability still remains as big concern as consequence of climate change impacts. In this area safe water sources and its infrastructure along with sanitation facilities are becoming threating due to gradually increasing salinity, tidal influence, unusual rainfall, and cyclone and storm surge. Through this project WaterAid Climate Resilience Programme will try to promote resiliency for WASH services with addressing rights for climate vulnerable people. Project will play a key role in local water and sanitation planning and implementation taking consideration of climate change impacts and adaptive capacity to cope with changing global phenomena. This requires huge capacity building support to community, piloting of appropriate technology and approach with effective engagement of local government and relevant stakeholders. Aligning with programmatic objectives, this project intended to achieve five specific changes: increase access to safe water and improved sanitation for poor and excluded in climate vulnerable area, proper hygiene practice among household members and school children improved, increase awareness on climate resilience and water safety compliance, improve resilience and inclusive WASH facilities at institutions and public places, improve leadership role of local government institutes in planning, coordinating and monitoring WASH services.

2. What?

Project theme – This project will promote resiliency for WASH services with addressing rights for climate vulnerable people in through building capacity and empowering community, local government and duty bearer to develop context specific technology, approach and adaptive planning in an integrated manner with multi-sectoral coordination.   Major activities that will be implemented are: Conducting study to asses and long term prediction of climate change impact on WASH sector in the project area; Exploring and introducing suitable and scalable climate adaptive WASH technology to ensure greater access to WASH services for climate vulnerable community;  Enhancing integrated adaptive planning leading by local government through vulnerability assessment and multi-sectoral coordination; Disseminating knowledge on WASH, CCA and DRR through capacity building to community and local government along with establishing a workable linkage

1. Why?

The northwest region of Bangladesh is drought prone area with its diverse landscape and semi-arid climate. The region has been experiencing recurrent below-average rainfall and thus lowering of groundwater levels in aquifers along with surface water flow reduction leading to water crisis for household, industry and agriculture. Apart from this the changed climatic condition has been posing a serious threat for which water crisis becoming more frequent, more intense, more spatially extensive, and of longer duration. A typical dry climate with comparatively high temperature (ranging from 25-45 degree Celsius) prevails here with severe water scarcity during 4-5 months of the year.Depending on its hydro-geology and aquifer materials, ground water of the project area is mainly recharged by the rainwater infiltration. A gradual declining of groundwater level during dry season without full recovery during wet season, delayed monsoon, less rainfall, drying rivers have been contributing to present water crisis. Groundwater level is continuously lowering at the rate of 1.37 ft/y in wet season and 0.72 ft/y in dry season. Recurrent shortage of surface water and ground water has posed several impacts that can be categorized as economic, social, environmental and hygienic. Water dependent sanitation options become unhygienic due to water scarcity and a large part of the poor and hard core poor population cannot afford to have expensive options regarding this. Most of the water management projects in this area mainly focus on flood control, drainage and agricultural production, ignoring availability of safe water for domestic consumption. Integration between integrated water resources management and WASH as human rights is largely absent in water projects. In this context, there is a need for an integrated community-based management process backed by improved knowledge, informed and skilled community members, LGIs and other duty bearers who are able to manage water project with equity, efficiency and sustainability. This project is built on the premises that acknowledge WASH as an integral part of IWRM approach, which provides a framework for action to adapt climate change impacts on water resources management. This indicates an urgent effort for resilient WASH facilities, local knowledge and awareness, innovative technology and community practices, capacity building, good governance and commitment from the stakeholders. The essence of such integration (WASH in IWRM) is clearly spelled out in SDG 6.  Implementation of the project will follow a community based bottom-up approach and takes sequenced steps to understand and respond to the challenges of the local context: first, understanding the ground reality through primary and secondary research; then capacity building and raising awareness in the community and instigating collective action through innovative participatory mechanisms; and finally, taking the lessons from these practices forward into local and national policy making and research. Following major activities will constitute the main building blocks of the project. Aligning with programmatic objectives, this project intended to achieve five specific changes: increase access to safe water and improved sanitation for poor and excluded in climate vulnerable area, proper hygiene practice among household members and school children improved, increase awareness on climate resilience and water safety compliance, improve resilience and inclusive WASH facilities at institutions and public places, improve leadership role of local government institutes in planning, coordinating and monitoring WASH services.

1. Why?

Several reports including World Bank 2015 warn that climate change will push an additional 100 million people back into poverty by 2030 and that South Asia will be hardest hit. This will be a major threat to achieving several SDGs, and not least goal 6 on water and sanitation. This is particularly concerning for the coastal areas of Southwest Bangladesh, where cyclones Sidr (2006) and Aila (2009) shattered infrastructures and livelihood opportunities, and many of those affected are yet to recover. Drinking water and sanitation scarcity is still severe following those disasters, leading to poor hygiene behaviour. This is having a severe impact on the livelihoods and health of communities in the project area, and in particular on women and girls.  During the dry season, women and girls travel 3 to 4 km to fetch water – water, which is not even safe to drink – leaving less time for income-generating activities and education. Furthermore, many families are left without male family members, who are to migrate to find work. Adding to this, high salt levels and hydrological conditions caused by climate change in coastal areas are creating technological challenges to the supply of safe water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH). Saline levels are particularly problematic in the project area, with levels increasing in both surface and groundwater. As a result, some technologies are no longer working and there is subsequently an acute need for new solutions that can withstand climate change. Proposed project will improve WASH security for 75,000 climate vulnerable people (70% of which are women and children) in coastal areas on the southwest of Bangladesh, by strengthening the resilience of WASH facilities. This will be achieved by testing and evolving climate resilient technologies; increasing sustainable access to safe drinking water and sanitation; improving hygiene behaviour; and increasing local capacity of communities and local governments to maintain facilities. This will contribute to Bangladesh achieving universal access to safe water and sanitation by 2030 (SDG6), which will in turn facilitate the achievement of  goals related to poverty (SDG1), good health (SDG3), quality education (SDG4), gender equality (SDG5). Aligning with programmatic objectives, this project intended to achieve five specific changes: increase access to safe water and improved sanitation for poor and excluded in climate vulnerable area, proper hygiene practice among household members and school children improved, increase awareness on climate resilience and water safety compliance, improve resilience and inclusive WASH facilities at institutions and public places, improve leadership role of local government institutes in planning, coordinating and monitoring WASH services.

  • Increase access to safe water and improve sanitation through the Installation or upgrading of context specific climate resilient water and sanitation technology at community, education and health institute.      
  • Enhancing integrated adaptive planning leading by local government through vulnerability assessment and multi-sectoral coordination

1. Why?

Bangladesh has made tremendous progress in dealing with open defecation (OD) and has recently achieved almost zero open defecation rate. However, to sustain the progress, the country need to invest more and find out ways to help people climb sanitation ladder. Currently, 62% of the population are using improved toilets. Bringing the remaining 38% to improve sanitation coverage is a challenge since they mostly fall under lowest economic strata of the society. Similarly, 13% people in the country is using unimproved source of drinking water. This population also needs support to move out from unimproved to improved sources of water.   Although microfinance has played a tremendous role in alleviating widespread rural poverty in the country, the applicability of microfinance to develop water and/or sanitation has not been tested yet. Since investing in water and sanitation does not provide any tangible benefit, and cash income to help poor people repay the loan, it might also be difficult for the poor people to adopt this strategy. For the same reason, water and sanitation has not been considered as a ‘product’ by the MFIs considering the associated repayment risk.   However, considering the long-term potential benefits of introducing microfinance for WASH development, WaterAid has tested a model whereby, demand for improved WASH has been created by the WASH facilitators in the community, and then link them with MFIs to access credit. However, considering WASH as a new product, and to minimize the repayment risk, the social mobilization part has to be more intensive compared to CLTS or other approaches. Emerging evidence suggest that the approach works well and can be scaled up. 

2. What?

Objective and Outcomes:  

The project will create demand for improved WASH services and promote microfinance services to help communities improve their household WASH facilities. The project will help MFIs to develop customised product and ingrain fairly easy terms of lending and repayment to attract potential clients who wish to improve their WASH facilities.  

Programmatic Approach:  

The project will intervene to arrange the provision of conditional loans for the poor households/ families for upgrading, reconstruction and maintenance of their WASH facilities (Water points/sources and toilets) to convert them into improved ones. Special loan product will be developed and given to poor households for establishing WASH facilities. The project will also work with the local level entrepreneurs to use different promotional mechanism to accelerate uptake of different WASH products.  This will be done to encourage the institution to operate this service and promote WASH as a product for microfinance. Alongside, awareness raising sessions to promote the use of hygienic and improves toilets will take place to raise and ensure the demand for the facility.   

Expected outcome:

the project will allow roughly 9,000 households to have access to safe water and improved sanitation. This will help them for timely repairs while also generating funds for major repairs or additional construction. The project is thus expected to contribute in the sustainability of the WASH interventions already implemented; while stimulating demand and supply for improved WASH solutions to many others who cannot afford it otherwise. Moreover, WASH in not yet considered as a product for micro finance. This project will be a pilot initiative for the MFIs to understand that WASH could be a product for investment through micro credit. By addressing the financial constraints the project will contribute to SDG-6 and help communities pass through the sanitation ladder. 

Estimated timeframe: 36 months

Estimated cost: GBP 300,000

1. Why?

By default, poor people who live in hard to reach areas are deprived under multiple indicators of inequality laying with economic, social and cultural reasons. An estimated 14% of the population in Bangladesh lacks access to safe drinking water, while 43% lacks access to improved sanitation[1]. Although there are some improvements in last two decades, vulnerable pockets remain, including those living in hard to reach terrains or suffering from socioeconomic marginalisation due to poverty, gender or disability. Those most deprived from access to WASH are groups that are already vulnerable in socioeconomic terms: children, women and adolescent girls, the elderly and infirm, persons with disabilities, and the extreme poor. Deprivation of water and sanitation rights for these groups are made manifold by the close linkages of water and sanitation with health and education[2]. Under critical geographic consideration Fulchori upazila under Gaibandha district marks as one of the disaster vulnerable upazilas at the northwest district under Rangpur Division. The division has the highest proportion of poor in the country, with 30% of its population classified as being in the lowest of five wealth categories[3]. Most of the communities characterised by chars, or riverine islands formed by sedimentation, where a predominantly poor or extreme poor population live at constant risk of flooding and erosion, with little access to services and health and education infrastructure. The challenges of this area are evident in the Hard-to-Reach ranking conducted by the World Bank’s Water and Sanitation Program (WSP), which ranks all 7 unions in Fulchari upazila of Gaibandha, as extremely or very hard to reach.

[1] WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme, Progress on drinking water and sanitation: 2012 update. [2] DeFrancis, Marc P. 2012. Economic impacts of inadequate sanitation in Bangladesh. 2. What?

As WASH deprivation experienced by the most marginalized groups demand immediate response, interventions adopt a service-delivery approach. However, limitations of an exclusively hardware-based service delivery approach, includes creating dependency, poor self-reliance, lack of ownership by local communities and rampant corruption. Unless processes are improved by way of bottom-up changes in the local power dynamics where the government acts to address the local demand from rights holders in an transparent and accountable manner, universal accessibility of WASH cannot be ensured. Experiencing from the last 03 years implementation of HRBA pilot and following district wide approach, the project will extend its working areas rest of the union of same sub district with massive engagement of LGIs, upazila administration and integrate health and education actors in realising WASH.

While there are wide complexity of issues that are leading the WASH deprivation of target communities, the project will focus to address the following pertinent causative factors:    

Lack of awareness of awareness of the community about the importance of WASH, its relation to health, the various WASH policies/programs of the government,  their rights/entitlements, and lack of critical awareness of how the system works  

Limited or no CSOs working with local communities on WASH issue from a ‘rights perspective'  

Lack of involvement of the local communities in the planning, implementation and monitoring of WASH programs including poor budgetary allocation for WATSAN services

Non-functioning WASH institutions, including lack of transparency, accountability and corruption

1. Why?

 Eighty seven percent people of Bangladesh have access to improved drinking water source, which is adjusted for Arsenic contamination in the ground water (JMP 2015). Despite progress on overall water coverage and achieving the MDG water target: access to safe water for all remains a challenge, particularly due to arsenic contamination. About 20 million people drink water contaminated with Arsenic. In terms of sanitation, Bangladesh has seen a remarkable reduction in open defecation of 33 percentage points from 1990 to 2015, to the current rate of 1% (JMP 2015). But it is to be noted that only 61% toilet can be categorized as improved toilet. And like the situation of water coverage, in hard to reach rural areas the percentage of improved toilet is much less than the national coverage.   Water, sanitation and hygiene are fundamental to health, and these are even more crucial for child health. There has been significant progress in reducing child mortality globally but infectious diseases remain a big challenge for children. Diarrhoea is one of the major causes of death of under 5 children and around 90% of diarrhoeal cases are caused by poor WASH condition. 50% of under-nutrition is due to lack of WASH, and under-nutrition is an underlying risk factor for around 30% of under-five deaths. Repeated episodes of diarrhoea can make under-nourished children predisposed to pneumonia. A lack of hygiene and sanitation and the associated diarrhoea also contributes to stunting and inhibited cognitive development in millions of children worldwide.   

Ground water, which is the mostly used drinking water in Gangni upazila, is heavily contaminated with Arsenic. According to DPHE (Department of Public Health Engineering) 60% of the water points (tube wells) are contaminated with Arsenic in Meherpur. Many people are using Arsenic contaminated water for drinking and other purposes, and many collect Arsenic free water from existing deep tube well in the area which are usually far from their home. This use of Arsenic contaminated water poses great health risk, especially Arsenic contamination causes incidence of Arsenicosis disease which sometimes turns into cancer. Already there are incidence of death from Arsenicosis disease and many are still suffering. And alarmingly there are no specialized health services to treat the Arsenicosis patients in government health centers. Condition of sanitation and hygienic practices are also very poor in this area.

1. Why?

This project is aimed at transforming the lives of teagarden workers by improving their access to safe drinking water, improved sanitation and hygiene behaviour. In a survey, in some selected tea gardens in the area, only 3.5% workers’ household had access to improved sanitation and 53.7% practiced open defecation. With a very little improvement in the overall situation over a decade, teagarden workers are still excluded from the mainstream population and are deprived of many essential services including access to improved WASH services. For the tea garden workers and their families, stream water was the source of water for drinking, cleaning and bathing. Moreover, they had to openly defecate in the same place near the stream due to lack of access to water which is the main source of contamination of the water they use for various domestic purposes. Their rights and needs are yet to be addressed by the duty bearers and their children still deprived of education and basic health care services. As a result, they also lack awareness menstrual hygiene management, waste disposal, environmental sanitation and the importance of WASH in health and family wellbeing.  The proposed project will be implemented in selected tea gardens of Sreemangal upazila of Moulvibazar district of Sylhet division in Bangladesh to support the excluded tea picker communities with WASH access and to help them raise their voice to claim WASH rights. The project will be implemented as a combination of service delivery and influencing. Advocacy initiatives will influence the local government and garden authorities to take more responsibility to ensure WASH facilities and access for teagarden workers. Challenges acknowledged but ignorance continued: WaSH deprivation of excluded community intensified by Islam, a paper presented in Asia Regional Sanitation and Hygiene Practitioners Workshop in Dhaka, Bangladesh.

2. What?

 Objective

The overall objective of this project is to improve health and social wellbeing of the poor and disadvantaged tea-pickers by providing them with the access to safe drinking water, improved sanitation and at the same time influencing their health and hygiene practices. Teagarden workers, being one of the marginalized communities are deprived of these basic facilities. The project will thus mainly focus on improving water supply and sanitation facilities in the communities where they live. More specifically the project is aimed at doing the following: ·     

Small-scale piped water network will be built and deep tube well will be constructed. This will ensure that they do not have to walk long distance to collect safe drinking water or use contaminated surface water. Existing unimproved and non-functional water points will also be renovated for use.   ·     

Communities and schools will be supported with improved sanitation facilities. Facilities provided in institutions will be disable-friendly and will have Menstrual Hygiene Management facilities in separate female chambers.   ·     

To ensure that the people understand and practice improved hygiene behaviour, such as hand-washing with soap at critical times and safe disposal of waste, awareness raising session and campaigns will be held. Social mobilisation will be carried out to influence their hygiene behaviour especially hand-washing practice with soap and menstrual health and hygiene management of adolescent girls and women of reproductive-age.   ·     

To further motivate and habituate to practice hand washing, the project will disburse hand washing devices in the communities.

The intervention will take place with the support from local government and tea-garden authorities and influence to strengthen the existing trends of coordination and engagement of local government, tea estate authorities for a sustainable WASH provision and management.

Expected outcomes

The project will transform lives of 3,000 teagarden workers and their family members including children with access to safe drinking water. More than 2,000 workers including their family members are expected to stop open defecation or use of unhygienic fixed-place defecation, following construction of improved sanitation facilities (toilets). The project will also rigorously motivate 2,500 community people irrespective of age and gender to adopt hand washing practices with soap at critical times. Hygiene motivation will also reach adolescent girls about improving their menstrual health and hygiene management. Apart from providing access to safe drinking water and improved sanitation facilities, the inclusion will bring an impact on family wellbeing and rights of the deprived teagarden workers in terms of protecting their human and social dignity, reducing incidence of water borne diseases, improved hygiene behaviour and sanitation practices.

Sustainability

Responsibility for operation and maintenance of the water options will be sold to the teagarden authorities through discussion and appreciations. They will allocate necessary resources for this purpose. A group of workers from among the beneficiaries will be trained on operation and maintenance of the facilities. They will take care of small repairing jobs. For major repairing, the community groups will be connected with local technicians and vendors who will attend the major maintenance work on call. Local government (Union Council) will be influenced to keep budgetary provision in their annual budget to provide sanitation subsidy (materials sus as ring, slab, sato pan etc) in the subsequent years. The workers will also demand contribution from the garden authorities to feed hardware maintenance activities that would be required in the subsequent years.

1. Why?

The urban poor living in slums and low-income communities in major cities and municipalities in Bangladesh are deprived of basic utility services including water and sanitation. These populations suffer disproportionately from poor WASH and associated shortfalls in healthcare, reflected in a number of health indicators including exposure to waterborne diseases, nutrition, maternal and child mortality, and healthcare seeking behaviour. Poor WASH access and health problems have a number of knock-on impacts on livelihood, productivity, income, and dignity. Growing morbidity leads to loss of work days, rising healthcare costs, and loss of productive time in collecting water or using sanitation facilities. However, it is evident that the provision of access to safe and adequate WASH can contribute to reducing the disease burden of the urban poor, and thus limit the overall impact on pubic and environmental health. Evidences suggest that improved water supply reduces diarrhoea morbidity by 21%, ascariasis by 29%, hookworm infection by 4%, trachoma morbidity by 27%, while additional improvement of drinking water quality would lead to a reduction of diarrhoea by 45%. Improved sanitation reduces diarrhoea morbidity by 37.5%.[1] Improved access to these services creates significant health outcomes[2] & economic benefits[3] & is considered to be one of the primary drivers of public health.[4]  The project aims to contribute to improved health and resilience of WASH-deprived urban poor living in slums and low income settlements of three cities and three municipal towns of Bangladesh. To achieve this goal, the expected outcomes of the project are: WASH deprivation and public health risks reduced among target communities through increased access to climate/ disaster resilient, community-based WASH services. Effectiveness of urban primary healthcare clinic sand service delivery in target cities and towns improved through WASH integration · Policy and practice on resilient WASH and its integration in health services improved for urban poor through evidence based and collective advocacy.

1. Why?

 In 2006, The Government of Tanzania adopted its Water Sector Development Programme (WSDP). Improving the quality of life for all its citizens, this 20-year plan with support from several Development Partners (DPs) seeks to meet basic water and sanitation targets under the United Nations' Millennium Development Goals. Phase 1 of the WSDP (2006-2014) highlighted the construction of 43,255 rural water points serving 10.8 million people and involving 183 Local Government Authorities (LGAs)[1]. Initiated in 2015, Phase 2 of the national program has committed to ensuring 80% of rural Tanzanians have access to a safe water supply, 75% have access to improved sanitation services, and 75% adopt best hygiene practices by 2021.

 

Despite progress on construction of new water schemes under WSDP I, it is estimated that only 46% of the rural population in Tanzania has access to an improved water source.[2]  Sustainability remains an overarching challenge for the rural water sub-sector in Tanzania. In 2015, a national water point mapping survey showed that out of Tanzania’s 86,946 rural water points, 54% were functional, 7% needed repair and 39% were non-functional.

 

The building blocks for sustainability, which ensure longevity and enable continuous water supply services, are multi-dimensional and have puzzled water sector practitioners for decades. WSDP I adopted a Community Driven Development (CDD) approach, where the community and its institutions, the so-called COWSOs (Community Owned Water Supply Organizations), were given full ownership of their rural water systems along with the associated management responsibility. Most rural water investment under WSDP I[3] went to diesel pumping (DP) schemes known for their high operation and maintenance (O&M) costs. This left communities with high O&M liabilities, some approaching the limit of their financial capacities. (It is nevertheless important to note that in the first years of the WSDP I, there were no viable alternatives to diesel generators in off-grid areas.) In addition to the high O&M costs, COWSOs often suffer from poor financial management including poor transparency and accounting mechanisms.

 

On the other hand, regional governments vested with the responsibility to oversee and hold LGAs to account are poorly resourced and have no incentive to act on behalf of the central government. Recognising the need for sound financial and technical management--which applies not only to water projects, but also to sanitation and hygiene initiatives alike--the government established the WASH Regional and Districts Teams. Although these teams exist in all regions and districts, they rarely meet to perform their designated functions.

 

Most donors including the World Bank and DFID are now opting for results-based financing (Payment by/for Results). Such initiatives often have an unintended consequence of triggering a perverse incentive in WASH service delivery, such that in order to get quick results, services are directed to the easiest-to-reach masses, thereby pushing the poorer, marginalized communities to the end of priority list. This means that without intervening, those who have the highest need (the marginalized 20% poorest) will be the last to be served.

 

In conjunction with this situation, CSOs in Tanzania, leaders and citizens have limited knowledge on government systems, low capacity to analyse information, and thereby challenge the authorities or status quo. Their engagement in high profile policy debates is still low and thus does not provide the necessary push and ideas for government to be continuously alert and responsive to citizen needs and aspirations in the WASH sector.[4] Even where the CSOs, leaders, and citizen have the knowledge, the WASH policy dialogue systems provide very limited opportunities for CSO and citizen participation, especially at regional and district levels.

 

While illustrating water challenges in a rural setting, technical and managerial capacity challenges exist across all components of the WASH sector. Recognising these critical challenges, as well as the goals of the Tanzania’s Water Sector Development Plan, this project proposes innovative technologies and management approaches to enhance the sustainability of WASH services--in both rural and urban settings--as well as to strengthen sector knowledge. 

2. What?

The purpose of project is two-fold:  1) to ensure access to sustainable and affordable water supply to rural communities, urban and peri-urban areas; and 2) to strengthen WASH rights awareness and accountability mechanisms at all levels including community, CSO, local government authority (LGA) and parliamentary. Specific objectives of this project include:

  • Addressing high O&M costs by demonstrating effective technologies with lower operational and maintenance cost (e.g., photovoltaic pumping system (PVPS))

  • Piloting electronic prepayment for water services to enhance transparency and accountability.

  • Promoting more sustainable WASH systems through behaviour change and behaviour-centred approaches. This change will occur on two fronts with: 1) communities and COWSOs recognising water supply as an important social and economic good that requires an effective commercial model to sustain, rather than being a mere service to be supplied, and 2) improved WASH as a result of behaviour change and adoption of improved sanitation options and hygiene best practices.

  • Piloting/implementing a comprehensive awareness and training package on Human Rights Based Approaches, which integrates knowledge of WASH rights, empowerment of community leaders to engage with government accountability mechanisms such as O&OD, and training of community representatives on local verification mechanisms including Community Score Cards.

  • Developing a comprehensive capacity-building package for CSOs based on understanding of WASH indicators, government commitments, performance monitoring, analysing results for transparent decision-making, and using ward, district and regional level platforms to support government in better use of their own data.

1.   Why?

WaterAid Uganda (WAU) country strategy 2016 -2021 pays particular attention to institutional efficiency and effectiveness of the Water Sanitation and Hygiene (WASH) sector. This is because strong institutions contribute to a country’s prospects of growth by reducing leakages resulting from inefficiencies.  Government plays a range of functions including regulation, licensing, arbitration, quality and coordination as well as engagement and management of private sector in the provision of WASH services. The current public service efforts for delivery of quality services is undermined by wastage, laxity, and limited responsiveness to citizens needs. WASH Sector Research and Development Project shall be implemented in conjunction with Ministry of Water and Environment to professionalise delivery of sustainable WASH services that meet the needs of the poor and marginalised especially women and girls. This shall focus on development of appropriate policies that are guided by research and execution of policies to neatly link policy and practice in WASH service provision.

 

2. What?

 

  In professionalising the WASH sector the project shall focus on delivering the following results 

-       Establishment of a functional water resources institute that offers continuous professional  development services for sector actors in relation to standards and guidelines 

-       Support capacity building for independent WASH sector regulator in development of guidelines, strategies and tools for planning, monitoring and reporting.

-       Establishment and capacity building of public water companies and User associations in the management and provision of customer oriented WASH services.

1.     Why?

Water, sanitation and hygiene are fundamental to health and are crucial in achieving the health outcomes. Although Uganda  has made some progress in reducing child mortality from 162 per 1,000 live births in 1990 to 54/1,000 live births in 2015 (DHS, 2015), the basic determinants for child health such as Water , sanitation and hygiene  are poor. The consequences of poor WASH service delivery in health care facilities are numerous. 15% of the patients develop one or more infections during hospital stay due to poor sanitation and hygiene conditions. An infection such as diarrhoea is the third biggest killer of children under five and almost 90% of cases of diarrhoea are caused by poor WASH services. 50% of under nutrition is due to lack of WASH services and nutrition is an underlying risk factor for about 30% of under- five deaths. Lack of access to safe clean water and sanitation may discourage mothers giving birth from the health care facility or lead to delay in seeking medical care.  While the Availability of  good WASH services reduce health care-related infections, increase trust and uptake of services, increase efficiency and decrease cost of service delivery and improve staff morale. Use of safe water, sanitation facilities and good hygiene can positively affect nutritional outcomes by addressing both immediate and underlying causes of malnutrition.

1.     Why?

Generally, WASH sector in Uganda stagnated in coverage and quality of services delivered for the last 10 years. As a result, over 10 million Ugandans lack safe water; 65% are not practicing improved hygiene behaviour; and about 8% of the total population practice open defecation. The sanitation situation is worse in informal settlements and in rural areas where 16% of the female headed households are reported to be using the bush for toilets, compared to 10% of their male counterparts (UBOS, WASH Gender and water statistics profile, 2012). This partly contributes to WASH related diseases such as diarrhoea that account for 8% of neonatal deaths (WHO: 2015).

In the Schools, the situation is not any different; according to the Uganda Water and Environment Sector Performance report for 2015, the average pupil to stance ratio stood at 1:67 as opposed to the expected 1:40, which was only achieved by 6% of the district; in the same report, access to hand washing facilities in schools was reported as being 38%. Many reports have also indicated that girls also fail to complete education due to lack of a decent toilet at school. This situation is exacerbated by Low policy prioritization that has led to a low budget allocation of 3% to the sector out of the required 6% plus the challenge of lack of clarity on the institution mandated to coordinate the school WASH. In healthcare facilities, while a clean environment and access to clean water are essential to reducing new-borns’ risk of infection, in Uganda, sepsis and diarrheal infection are responsible for 7% and 1% neonatal death respectively. While comparatively, 8% of under-five children are reported to die of diarrheal annually. Indirect causes such as lack of water, sanitation and hygiene facilities in health centers account for 17% of child maternal deaths, with 9% of the sepsis directly attributed to poor hygiene during delivery.

The organization will facilitate awareness campaigns to enable the learners to understand their rights, demand, pay for, utilize and sustain school WASH services. WAU will influence non-state actors (including the private sector) to plan and effectively deliver and account for inclusive and sustainable WASH services. Research on inequalities in access will be conducted to generate raise for awareness campaign using, especially, media platforms to advocate for better WASH services.

1. Why?

 

The Government of Uganda Water Adaptation Strategy Report (GoU, 2011) notes the main economic sectors that are directly affected by water supply and variability include agriculture and livestock, fisheries and aquaculture, forestry and tourism. Complementary sectors include lake transport and energy production. Social sectors that are impacted include health and nutrition as well as water and sanitation. Seasonal and spatial variability of precipitation causes specific problems as the country encompasses both humid and semi-arid areas (MWE, 2014). In particular, existing evidence suggests that droughts are becoming more frequent and more severe with major events occurring in 2001, 2002, 2005 and 2008 (UNDP, 2013). Future damages are difficult to predict as the agricultural sector faces two opposing trends. On the one hand yields will decline for some crops due to climate change and on the other the production will increase as a result of better techniques to manage inputs and investment in adaptive agriculture including water for production. Water is a key base for almost all human activity and the consequences of these changes are very significant throughout the economy and society but more so to the poor and vulnerable groups such as women and girls.

2. What?

 

WAU’s planned interventions will be contributing to realising national aspirations and targets for the next 15 years as spelt out in the country’s Climate change costed Implementation Strategy as well as the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) which commit Uganda internationally to respond to climate change. WAU will therefor focus on Adaptation actions in the sectors of water and sanitation as a way of building long term resilience especially for women and girls but also increase productivity and mitigation of climate change induced conflicts. WAU’s focus areas in this project will include the following:

1. Climate change knowledge and learning for long term resilience

  1. Generating and disseminating evidence on climate variability and its impacts on water resources and WASH services through research and vulnerability assessments

  2. Developing and promoting disaster warning systems to strengthen the ability of institutions to mitigate or respond flexibly and rapidly to climate change and related disasters

  3. Designing and promoting water supply and sanitation designs which are less vulnerable to variations and disasters such as floods and droughts as well as the increasing competition for water use.

  4. Promoting Integrated Water Resources Management (including underground water resources) as well as contingency planning for extreme floods and droughts

  5. Support institutional and human capacity building in water resource use, development and management

     

2. Strengthening the enabling environment for climate change resilience

  • Strengthening climate change education, training and public awareness about existing options to build resilience and adaptation

  • Performance monitoring of the level of action and inaction in implementation of the national and global climate change agenda in the Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC)

  • Regular monitoring and reporting to on climate change mainstreaming and financing at national, sub-national and global levels

3. Where?

 

The project will be implemented in the cattle corridor of Uganda which stretches from North East to South West. The target population in this corridor largely depends on livestock and are greatly affected by climate change as a result of prolonged droughts which result into water stress and scarcity of pastures for the animals. IN addition to Kampala, seven districts will be sampled from the cattle corridor, based on vulnerability criteria that also promotes cross-learning and scale up of innovative resilience building technologies. Pastoralists are among the poorest Ugandans, with high rates of infant and maternal mortality, low levels of literacy, and limited political participation yet the livestock sector contributes about 08 per cent of GDP. Climate change has exacerbated resource scarcity and placed tremendous pressure on the pastoralist livelihoods and food security of the people in the cattle corridor, thereby increasing the potential for conflict over resources mainly water, land and pastures.

 

4. Who?

 

WaterAid will work with the Climate Change Department of the Ministry of Water and Environment, UN bodies such as FAO and UNDP currently implementing climate change response actions in the selected region as well as selected local and international civil society organisations and development partners such as ActionAid, WWF, Oxfam, and Care International in Uganda, GIZ and SIDA among others. WAU will build alliance with civil society platforms and networks at national, regional and global levels to pursue an inclusive climate change agenda for Uganda that observes the rights of women and girls.

1. Why?

The Country’s groundwater chemistry is highly variable and variations are seen between different types of water source and between different rock types (regolith or crystalline bedrock).  Many with low hardness are described as aggressive and are acidic (pH <7). These have the capability to corrode ferrous casings and pumps in tube wells and have led to impaired water quality (especially iron) and maintenance problems. Also, Fluoride and Salinity (chloride concentrations of several thousand mg/l) continued to be the main inorganic groundwater quality problems in the Country.  Despite these water-quality problems, groundwater remains the main source of safe drinking water contributing about 80% of water supply in the rural Uganda. Furthermore, faecal coliforms have been found in several surface and ground waters in rural, large towns and urban. This is attributed to the poor environmental sanitation (inappropriate solid and wastewater management). Whereas, the sector reported improved compliance to waste water discharge permits conditions from 52% to 54%, there is still high levels of nutrients and organic matter from urban and industrial point sources, resulting in eutrophication problems and oxygen deficiencies. Compliance of BOD5 and TSS in treated wastewater stands at only 46% and 45% respectively[1]. This implies that, NWSC facilities, factories and industries are still unable to comply with the National standards for Wastewater discharge in its totality.  This is attributed to inadequate treatment plants with limited efficiency and capacity but also ineffective regulations to improve and manage quality of service. This is affecting mainly the quality of urban water leading to increased treatment costs.

Access to portable water remains at 67% and 71% in rural and urban Uganda respectively. This implies that, a significant Country’s population (33% in rural, 29% in Urban) is still unserved with water service. This has stagnated for over five years and is expected to reduce further with the current sector reform to improve level of service and also the commitment made in the Vision 2040 which requires water to be within 200m from the homesteads as opposed to the 1000m in rural. The functionality rate continued to reduce in rural from 88% to 86% while Urban and water for production stands at 94% and 95% respectively. Service fails most often due to high pressure on the limited resources, inappropriate technologies, poor construction standards and management systems.  In recognition of the need for IWRM, the Government, through the National Water Policy 1999, established an Inter-Ministerial Committee (IMC) on Water. IMC has the mandate to focus on the interactions between water, environment, economy and society, and the situations of conflict and overuse that may arise. However, little has been done to institutionalize joint action for water resources management which also includes water quality management.

 

2. What?

Considering the importance of water supply to the socio-economic development of the country and the need to match the increasing water needs resulting from the growing population, industrialisation and uncontrolled environmental degradation, the project will adapt specific delivery approaches to accelerate water supply coverage and improve functionality of water sources. These include;

 

a)    Generate evidence on contribution of water supply and resources to the Country’s economy

ü  Research on economic dimensions of water systems -This will focus on not only how water institutions can be financially viable enterprises, but also how economic analyses can be and are being used to justify water allocation decision-making. This will also involve the development of a decision support tool that will predict the economic impact of a disruption of water service to local communities and the entire population. The analysis will investigate the impact on local and regional economies due to both water service disruptions and subsequent recovery actions.  The desired outcome is a tool that will assist water utility operators, as well as local and state governments, in making more informed decisions.

b)    Contribute towards strengthening Water Institutions through Research and demonstrations of appropriate inter-linkages approach such as IWRM for improved and sustained quality of services

ü  Contribute towards the review and development of adequate framework for effective regulation of Urban and Small towns Sub-sector to improve compliance to water and environmental quality standards and performance monitoring.

ü  Develop a robust evidence base and understanding of the complex and multi-faceted causes which underlie the current rapid high failure rates of many groundwater supplies in the country and entire Africa, so that future WASH investments can develop more sustainable groundwater-based water supplies.

ü  Research and test appropriate WRM options which will include socio-economic aspects, technological options for water supply with multiple uses, wastewater treatment (centralized versus decentralized systems), catchment management and removal of toxic inorganic chemistry such as Fluoride in groundwater

ü  Demonstrate Innovative technologies to ensure a greener and more sustainable food production. This will be done by implementing efficient irrigation strategies; reuse of drainage water and use of water resources of marginal quality; produce smarter ways to use fertilizer and water.

c)    Strengthen sector engagements and sharing of learnings

ü  Organize and facilitate Regional and National Water Resources Management symposium

ü  Document and share experiences and learnings in different fora such as international, Regional and National conferences/workshops

Estimated cost – The project will cost an estimate of about UGX 6,300,000,000= (Six Billion, Three Hundred Million Only) over the next 5 years. Only UGX 300,000,000= have been secured. The difference of UGX 6 billion is unsecured.

Estimated timeframe – The project will be implemented over a period of 60 months (5 years).

Existing WaterAid experience – WaterAid will build on its experience in developing and managing water resources but will pay key attention to the key drivers of water security, research and demonstrate appropriate technologies and models for water management as well as institutional strengthening.

3. Where?

The project will target the population in Metropolitan Kampala, Karamoja and Teso in North eastern Uganda. The two regions of Teso and Karamoja are semi-arid. The population is predominantly pastoralists (practice small-scale agriculture and livestock farming). Over the past years, the regions experienced a reduction in productivity yields, declined water levels, long drought leading to drying up of some water sources, floods and deteriorating water quality. The regions has perennial rivers and with rainfall varying highly, both spatially and temporally, water dries up quickly after the seasonal rains, leaving few sources of water pressurized. With the research components, the project will be flexible to work in other parts of the Country for purpose of having representative samples.
